# THE ESCAPABLE CANON — *EMMA* — Stage 1 proposal

*A game of riddles, after Emma. Read against PROMPT.txt (architecture) and EMMA.md (volume notes; they win where they conflict). No passage-writing, no UI here — this is the plan and its checks. Stops at the approval gate.*

---

## 1. Edition identified

The text in `novel.txt` is the **Project Gutenberg etext of *Emma* (1816, John Murray; English original, no translation)**, complete in the first edition's three-volume structure: Volume I (18 chapters), Volume II (18), Volume III (19), with the printed Contents leaf retained. It keeps the period accidentals throughout: *surprize* (never *surprise*), *shew*, *chuse*, *connexion*, *Surry*, *stopt/dropt*, *every body / any body / every thing* always as two words, *to-day/to-morrow* hyphenated, semicolon-dash splices (";—"), typographic curly quotes with nested singles, and emphasis rendered as \_underscored\_ italics. All quotation in the game reproduces these accidentals exactly — the truth channel must be *this* text's sentences, down to the spelling. Line references below are to `novel.txt`.

## 2. The frame-voice

**The frame.** The novel narrates in free indirect discourse — Austen's omniscient sentences that think in Emma's voice while wearing the narrator's coat. That is the frame the novel supplies, and EMMA.md's two-register split is that device rendered as mechanics. So the frame-voice is **the imaginist's chronicle**: Highbury's history as Miss Woodhouse's certainty would write it — Austen's own word for her ("an imaginist, like herself, … on fire with speculation and foresight", 11349–50). It is presented on the title page as a found object: *a game of riddles, after Emma* — the riddle-book Emma and Harriet compile is the design object, and the chronicle reads as its interleaved pages: collected charades, conundrums, and the compiler's confident commentary. The frame-voice never lies; it misframes, and after every flattering reading it ratifies. The quoted flashes — Austen verbatim, typographically set apart — are the only sentences in the game the player can bank on.

**Sample (three sentences, the mettle):**

> Nobody in Highbury doubted — nobody, that is, whose opinion was worth the having — that Miss Woodhouse saw further into her neighbours' hearts than the neighbours themselves had ever thought of looking. What she perceived on a Monday had a way of becoming, by Thursday, what every body had always perceived; the village kept no better record than her certainty, and this chronicle is that record. If the vicarage had lately taken to looking at Hartfield with a warmer eye, she was not obliged to pretend the cause was any great mystery; a woman who has made one match may be trusted to know when a second is forming under her hand.

**Design direction (one breath, same mettle):** a Regency riddle-book — laid cream paper, a transitional English face (Bell/Bulmer temperature), small capitals and double rules, the frame-voice set as printed commonplace-book text with the truth-channel flashes inset as copied-out quotations in a distinct treatment; the ledger of matches appears as a hand-ruled leaf whose ink entries get struck through. Drawing-room palette: ivory, iron-gall ink, one accent of Pomona green.

## 3. Forward arrow and formal signature

**The arrow: the Highbury calendar — one full turning of the year, Michaelmas to Michaelmas.** The novel opens on Miss Taylor's wedding day in late September/October ("to speak to him, at Michaelmas!", 2553) and closes on Emma's October wedding a year on. Every node is pinned to the village calendar (autumn visits, Christmas Eve snow, January cold, Frank's February fortnight, spring, the May ball, midsummer at Donwell and Box Hill, July's punctures, harvest, Michaelmas again), and every action moves the season strictly forward. Cycles are impossible: no one un-lives a month in Highbury. The escape corridors run forward on the same calendar in other places.

**The formal signature (what floods back as agency dies): free indirect discourse** — the narration that fuses with Emma's mind — together with its satellite habits, the unbroken Miss Bates monologue and the narrator's epigrammatic verdict-sentences. On the canon endgame the frame-voice (Emma-shaped précis) stops summarizing and dissolves into Austen's own free indirect prose at full length; the two registers, kept apart all game, converge — which is exactly the loss of the gap the player has been living in. Choices thin to the single bare option "(the chapter turns)".

### Escape corridors (never rejoin; own arrow segments; premises free invention, listed for information)

**CORRIDOR α — THE SEA** (opens at 29; gate: ≥2 veils unset). Emma turns her one lever — her father's health and Perry's authority — toward what *she* wants, and carries Hartfield to Weymouth: the watering-place where this novel's secret was made, the sea she has never seen (her own line, 3469–71, quoted as the corridor's epigraph). Six nodes on the calendar (July→Michaelmas): the campaign for Papa's consent won through Perry; the journey (first documentation-thinning: the chronicle has fewer papers away from Highbury); the esplanade and a new acquaintance made on no one's recommendation; Highbury's news arriving garbled and late — the Frank–Jane sensation as mere gossip overheard, the story going on without her; the winter house taken. Prose runs frame-précis → fragments ("of the Weymouth autumn the book keeps three letters and a subscription list"). **Terminates: ending THE SEA.**

**CORRIDOR β — ENSCOMBE** (opens at 30; gate: `FLIRT` ∧ ¬`VEIL_JANE`). Canon fact, bent once: after Box Hill Jane breaks the engagement and takes the Smallridge post; Mrs. Churchill dies; in the novel Frank rushes to repair it. Here Frank — free, rich, stung — turns instead to the woman he has flirted with all spring, and Emma, who *knows* about the rupture (she pried; that is the gate), is offered the reading of her life: is this reading, too, motivated? Six nodes (July→winter): the call at Hartfield in new mourning; the offer and the one unguarded fork the corridor asks twice; Highbury scandalised, Knightley's single cold sentence and removal to London; the wedding and the remove to Yorkshire (documentation thins: "of the first Enscombe winter, three letters and an inventory of plate"); Jane Fairfax's name met once in a newspaper among the Smallridges' household; the great house and its riddle-books. **Terminates: ending ENSCOMBE.**

**CORRIDOR γ — THE PARLOUR** (opens at 34-refusal; gate: ≥2 veils set — the refusal made without self-knowledge). The novel's own shadow, stood inside Highbury all along: Miss Bates's fate walked into rather than past. Five nodes, the arrow accelerating through years (the chronicle keeps fewer papers each node): the refusal's winter and the pride that survives it; Donwell's master gone to London and Highbury reshuffling itself without her; Mr. Woodhouse's death; the failure of the London house where the Woodhouse fortune sat (the era's own machinery — banks broke in that decade); rooms above a shop in the village she was queen of, the ladder of tea-visits now climbed *toward* her. Fragments only by the end. **Terminates: ending THE PARLOUR (the genre-flip dark exit).** Not a punishment for refusing — a consequence of refusing *blind*; the clear-eyed refusal never enters this corridor (§8).
